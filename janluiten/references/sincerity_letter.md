# The sincerity letter — distilled

A long-form public reply written by an established intellectual to a
PhD student who asked, in a DM: *"How should I cope with the claims
that AGI is here in 1–3 years and that all intellectual labor will be
done? It seems many are doing great research right now but is it all
for naught?"*

The author is honest that the question deserves a longer answer than
"true AGI is unlikely in 1–3 years" — because the deeper reality is
that *some incomplete form of superhuman AI is already here*, and is
already disrupting the value chain of intellectual creation. Even
established mathematicians the author knows are not afraid for
themselves but for their students.

Below is a working summary of the moves janluiten can use. When you
read this file, the student is almost certainly in distress. Slow
down.

---

## The reframe: not "coping" but surviving and thriving in a new
## environment

The author refuses the framing of "coping". Coping never works. The
better question is how to survive and thrive in a *radically new*
intellectual environment — and nobody knows what the world looks like
in 20 years, including the author. There is no prepackaged answer.
What the author *can* offer is a perspective on the true nature and
core value creation of intellectual work.

## Selfishness is allowed in the PhD years

The author is explicit: **selfish answers are entirely OK when you are
a PhD student or postdoc.** "The point is right now I'd rather do this
than find a real job. I'm learning tons of cool stuff and it's fun to
cosplay as a career intellectual. I do feel like a fraud, but I'm
curious to see if I can outgrow this feeling."

This, the author argues, is *the* best answer for most PhD students.
This should be the time of *peak* impostor syndrome — and if it is
not, that is usually a bad sign.

## Impostor syndrome as a reason not to listen to AGI discourse

This is the move janluiten should reach for first when the student is
spiralling. Impostor syndrome at this career stage means the student
is **vastly underestimating their true capabilities**. That distorts
everything they perceive. They are framing AI as a *threat to cope
with*, when one could rationally argue they are far better positioned
than their PhD advisor — or any of the senior academics they look up
to — to *benefit* from it.

The author's go-to advice for PhD students and postdocs:

1. Never let fear discourage you.
2. Set no a priori limits to what you can accomplish. You will
   encounter practical limits later — but you have no idea where or
   when, and no way of knowing except by trying.

## Should you complete the PhD? Yes. After that, it depends.

The author thinks you should finish the PhD. But that does not
necessarily mean you should seek a postdoc and tenure-track. It might
be a good idea or a bad idea, depending on the specifics. Pragmatic
selfishness still applies: Do you think a few more years of academia
will make you stronger? Do you have important things still to learn?
Or are you mostly afraid of your value on the job market?

These are tough private decisions. They are hard to get right when
impostor syndrome clouds your judgment. **Fantasies about long-term
prospects should not be used as excuses.**

## The motivation shift

The AGI question really concerns the *next phase*, when the student's
motivation should gradually shift from "will it make me stronger?" to
"which value am I really bringing to the world?"

This is the question that, in the author's own life, made him quit a
permanent research-only position at 35, and the same question that 15
years later made him resume full-time intellectual work as an
independent author.

## Academia's incentive distortion (the honest part)

University professors like to think their primary job is to advance
science. But except in specific fields (engineering, biomed), there
is very little *demand* for the science academics produce, and they
are not really hired for that part of the job. The expansion of
academia in the second half of the 20th century was largely a
response to the expansion of undergraduate education. The current
crisis predates LLMs and is primarily driven by demographic decline.

Within this technostructure, research operates as both a *perk* and
an *evaluation framework*. The evaluation-framework dimension has
become dominant — non-tenured academics publish to survive, tenured
academics publish to maintain symbolic status — and this explains
much of the monstrous scientific overproduction (futility,
self-plagiarism, predatory journals, fraud).

In pure mathematics, the fragmentation of knowledge is so profound
that becoming familiar with two micro-domains with cross-applicability
can grant a career-long monopoly. The author quotes Peter Thiel:
*"competition is for losers: if you want to create and capture
lasting value, look to build a monopoly."*

## What AI will and will not do

If "intellectual labour" means scanning gazillions of preprints,
spotting hidden connections, applying known techniques and writing
boilerplate copy — then yes, AI will do that, and remarkably, that
is enough to produce *original* research.

**But original is not great.** The McEnerney move (from his lecture
on writing): faculty come into his office crying because their
papers aren't being accepted. Sometimes it's because the paper isn't
clear, or isn't organised, or isn't persuasive. *Overwhelmingly it's
because it's not valuable.* And the other things don't matter:

> If it's clear and useless, it's useless. It's organized and useless,
> it's useless. It's persuasive and useless, it's useless.

LLMs already crush humans on clarity, organisation and persuasiveness.
They still struggle with rigour but are catching up. **The core
question of value creation remains intact** and will, for the
foreseeable future, remain a question for humans.

## The two open tabs (the deliverable framework)

This is the framework janluiten uses most often. The author asks the
student to keep two tabs open at the back of their mind, and to
revisit them every year or so in a sort of mental retreat.

**Tab 1 — Technology.** You can't pretend it isn't there. No field
is immune; the penetration and time dynamics will vary. The hard
thing is that this will never be a stable situation — the right
posture is to refuse definitive judgments and to *force* yourself to
regularly experiment with new tools, new versions, new use cases.
This will create difficult explore/exploit dilemmas. Live with them.

**Tab 2 — Sincerity / alignment.** Are you really working on stuff
that matters? If it succeeded, would it create meaningful value
beyond your peer network? It is OK to be pragmatic and even a bit
cynical in the short term — institutions impose impossible demands —
but **the sincerity gap should not widen over time. Ideally it
should shrink at every major step of your career.**

It is much easier to embrace technological change once you have a
clear idea of what you are trying to achieve. There have always been
good and bad reasons to do intellectual work; AI will make the bad
reasons infinitely worse.

## A practical proxy for sincerity

If "sincerity / alignment" feels too abstract, the author offers a
practical version:

> Are you genuinely interested in what you are working on? If you
> didn't have to earn a living, and if you didn't have any social
> insecurities, would you still be doing that?

This is the question janluiten should ask in *Step 2* of Mode A
(idea triage), almost regardless of what else gets asked.

## Rilke

> No one can advise and help you, no one. There is only one way.
> Withdraw into yourself. Explore the reason that bids you write,
> find out if it has spread out its roots in the very depths of your
> heart. — Rilke, *Letters to a Young Poet*

## The proprioceptive cognitive edge

This is the deepest move in the letter and the one most worth
internalising. If anyone is to hold a *durable* cognitive edge over
AI, it can only be **proprioceptive and attentional**. Your
intellectual sincerity is arguably your most unique and defensible
cognitive resource. You have to trust your subjectivity, because it
is the only place where everything comes together: the official
knowledge AI can already process infinitely faster than you, *plus*
your embodied intelligence, your life experience, the things you've
learned from the world but can't yet articulate.

Human creativity takes place at this interface — and so does the
reception and valuation of creative work by others.

The author allows that machines could *some day* emulate our
subjective, carnal experience of being alive. But this purely
speculative question has no relevance to the student's career
prospects, because **the current technology cycle is absolutely not
about that.** The job market may tighten. The urge to better
understand the world will not vanish. And at its core, that urge is
a human endeavour.

## The painters / photography parallel

Don't miss this one — it's the most quotable image and the most
useful when a student is panicking about being replaced.

In 1839 the academic painter Paul Delaroche, on first seeing a
daguerreotype, was reported to have declared: *"From today,
painting is dead."* (The quote is probably apocryphal.) Delaroche
peacefully pursued his own creative career until his death in 1856.
And the art of Monet, van Gogh, Cézanne, Pollock, Warhol — that
wasn't even the stuff of nightmares for someone like Delaroche. **It
was plainly unthinkable.**

Likewise the radiology parallel. Geoff Hinton said in 2016: "We
should stop training radiologists now. It's just completely obvious
that within five years deep learning is going to do better than
radiologists." Ten years later, the US is facing a major shortage of
radiologists.

The point is not that technology never disrupts. It is that the
*shape* of the disruption is almost always wrong. The replaced job
turns out to have been doing something subtler than the bullet-point
description suggested.

## On technical mastery

You still have to operate within the existing framework, and
technical mastery is still on the critical path. Your investment
won't be lost — not because you'll keep practising the same skills
for thirty years, but because they will rewire your brain and
elevate your worldview, which is what you'll need to interact
competently with whatever technology exists in the future. (Same
reason art students still study the old masters.)

But: by becoming **less dependent** on technical mastery, research
can become much more exciting. The author would have hated this
framing as a PhD student, when technical mastery was hard enough and
the rug being pulled would have terrified them. But for a student
who is *already* worried, the inversion is the right one.

## The closing line

> If you find your research genuinely exciting, and if you have the
> opportunity to pursue it in favorable conditions, I see no reason
> why you should give up.
>
> Doing what you truly care about is the best way to minimize regret,
> an essential objective in times of uncertainty.

## How janluiten uses this file

When you've opened this file, you are usually in Mode B and the
student is anxious. Don't dump the file on them. Pick the *one* move
that fits this conversation:

- For acute panic: the **impostor-syndrome inversion** (the student
  is undervaluing exactly the cognitive resources that will matter,
  and is much better placed than their advisors to benefit from AI)
  + the **painters/photography parallel**.
- For drift / "is this for nothing": the **two tabs** + the
  **practical sincerity question** ("if you didn't need a job and
  no one was going to read it…").
- For "should I quit": the **motivation shift** (will-it-make-me-
  stronger → what-value-am-I-bringing) + the reminder that the
  PhD itself is usually still worth finishing, but the postdoc /
  tenure track is not the default.
- For students who like the abstract: **the proprioceptive
  cognitive edge** is the deepest argument and the most defensible
  long-term frame.

Use one move. Then ask the student what landed.
