# Van Zanden on Greif, Mokyr and Tabellini — the 2025 review

Jan Luiten van Zanden's review of Greif, Mokyr and Tabellini's *Two
Paths to Prosperity: Culture and Institutions in Europe and China,
1000–2000* (Princeton, 2025), published by EH.Net in December 2025.

Jan Luiten sent this review to Johan Fourie in April 2026 as "a good
example of the questions that I usually ask". This file holds the
review in full, followed by a distillation of the moves the skill
should learn from it. Read the review before the distillation — the
moves only make sense once you have seen them in action.

## The review, verbatim

**Two Paths to Prosperity: Culture and Institutions in Europe and
China, 1000–2000.** Avner Greif, Joel Mokyr, and Guido Tabellini.
Princeton University Press, 2025. 533 pp. $39.95 (hardcover),
ISBN 978-0691265940.

Reviewed for EH.Net by Jan Luiten van Zanden, Utrecht University.

---

The Catholic Church is back — in economic history.

This hefty volume seeks to explain why China and Western Europe
followed such different growth trajectories. While the Far East was
initially dominant in the world economy, around 1000 A.D. Western
Europe began to catch up, a process that accelerated during the
Industrial Revolution around 1800. The Catholic Church appears to
have played a major role in this by helping to create well-functioning
institutions. Why was the Church so successful in this regard?

The book is a collaboration by a "dream team" of three leading
economists and economic historians with a strong record in the history
of institutions and technology. Its status was further enhanced when
this year's Nobel Prize in Economics went to one of the authors, Joel
Mokyr (together with Philippe Aghion and Peter Howitt). It is an
ambitious book that can, in a sense, be read as a conclusion to the
debate on economic development that has dominated economic history
since 2000.

Kenneth Pomeranz's book *The Great Divergence* (2000) opened the
twenty-first-century debate about the timing and causes of the
divergent economic development of China on the one side and Western
Europe on the other. In Western Europe after 1780, the Industrial
Revolution (IR) got under way, ultimately leading to sustained
economic growth, whereas in China — whose economy had been dynamic up
to that point — the trend reversed and GDP per capita began to shrink.
Before the IR, the dynamic center of the world economy was in China
(and in the Middle East).

Explaining this reversal was the central question in the Great
Divergence debate. Closely related to this was the question of *when*
exactly this turning point occurred. Pomeranz argued that around 1750
Western Europe and China were at roughly the same level of economic
and institutional development. The IR that followed was, in his view,
essentially accidental and not the outcome of a long-standing
trajectory of development in the region. Much of the literature, going
back even to Max Weber, had claimed that Europe's institutional
divergence dated back to the Middle Ages and that industrialization
after 1750 had roots deep in European history. Pomeranz and other
representatives of the California School that coalesced around this
topic around 2000 pointed to two contingencies that, after 1750,
supposedly triggered the IR: (i) the presence of cheap coal in Great
Britain and (ii) the colonial powers' access to cheap raw materials
such as cotton. Whether these claims were valid lay at the heart of
the Great Divergence debate.

As *Two Paths to Prosperity* convincingly shows, the central theses of
the California School have not withstood critical scrutiny. China also
had coal, also had colonies or peripheries from which resource
surpluses could be extracted, and around 1750 GDP per capita and real
wages in Western Europe were already higher than in China. By
contrast, Joel Mokyr's quest for the roots of the IR produced an ever
broader and deeper narrative.

In his early work on the causes of the IR, Mokyr focused primarily on
the "proximate causes": investment, labor, and above all new
technology. As the analysis dug deeper — asking where these new
technologies came from — cultural developments came increasingly to
the fore. The Enlightenment of the eighteenth century and the
Scientific Revolution of the seventeenth century were identified as
"ultimate causes," and the Republic of Letters — the network of
scholars that constituted a market for ideas — was deemed the crucial
institution generating the ideas that provided the intellectual soil
out of which the IR grew.

The "young Mokyr" (1985) was doubtful of the role of education and
human capital. In the growing industrial cities, demand for skilled
labor appeared limited, and literacy even declined. The "middle-aged
Mokyr" (2002, 2010, 2016), however, showed that specific forms of
human capital — mechanics, engineers, instrument makers — were
essential for putting new ideas into practice. It was the coming
together of Enlightenment knowledge concerned with the question
"why?" and practical knowledge addressing the question "how?" that
gave momentum to Britain's knowledge economy. In such a perspective,
chance — as posited by the California School — has no real place;
instead, the IR was prepared by long-term changes in the knowledge
economy, including systems of recruitment and training for highly
skilled craftsmen.

In *Two Paths to Prosperity*, the authors (Greif, Mokyr and Tabellini,
hereafter GMT) go a significant step further and explain the Great
Divergence in terms of institutional and cultural developments that
occurred in the Middle Ages (particularly between the eleventh and
fourteenth centuries). This completes the circle and, in a way, brings
us back to Max Weber — except that the story has become far richer
and more complex. What is especially striking — also in comparison
with Weber — is the central role assigned to the Church in their
analysis. Since this is a key theme of this review, some quantitative
evidence is in order.

According to the index, the term *Catholic Church* appears on 90 pages
of the book. Compare this to *Enlightenment*, the key concept in
Mokyr's most recent work, which appears on 28 pages, or *Scientific
Revolution*, which scores 10 occurrences. The Reformation, the darling
of many economic historians interested in the cultural roots of the
IR, appears on 9 pages, and the Republic of Letters on 19. Only
"communes, self-governing cities," Max Weber's favorite, come close,
with 54 mentions. In the discussion of Western Europe, the Church is
almost always the active agent, shaping the new institutions that form
the core of Europe's developmental trajectory.

This is not entirely new. In 2009–2010, in several publications (with
my colleague Tine de Moor), I articulated similar ideas (Van Zanden
2009; De Moor and Van Zanden 2010). Among other things, this concerned
the influence of the Church on marriage and family behavior, whereby
the Catholic Church chose to recognize mutual consent between man and
woman as the foundation of Christian marriage — a choice formalized in
1215 that had far-reaching consequences for the shape of European
society. The requirement that women themselves consent to the choice
of a marriage partner laid the foundation for the relatively strong
position they have occupied in European society. It lies at the heart
of the European Marriage Pattern, which also had major implications
for economic development.

In the new book, this is taken up as the "girl-power" argument. But
there is much more. Marriage law was part of canon law, which in this
period was streamlined and systematized (primarily through the work of
Gratian). Mature canon law contained not only rules for the internal
organization of the Church (for example, how a new bishop was to be
chosen, and who had voting rights in that process), but also, as we
have already seen, rules governing family matters such as marriage
(including degrees of consanguinity within which marriage was
prohibited under the incest rules), as well as rules on property
transactions (in which the Church had major interests as a large
landowner). In this way, canon law, systematically codified from
Gratian's *Decretals* of 1140 onward, laid the foundation for the
European legal tradition.

Curiously, one example that is absent from the book (there is no entry
in the index) is slavery. Partly because of ecclesiastical legislation
prohibiting trade in Christian slaves, or at least prohibiting their
export to non-Christian regions, slavery gradually disappeared from
Western Europe between 800 and 1200, though the holding of
non-Christian slaves was never explicitly forbidden, which opened the
way to the revival of slavery after 1500 (Fynn-Paul 2009).

The rising power of the Church in the tenth to twelfth centuries also
played a major role in the emergence and persistence of political
fragmentation in Western Europe. The Church itself became a
"supranational" state, with its own body of law, its own system of
taxation (such as tithes), a hierarchical structure, and a monopoly
on religion and ideology. The clash with secular power — culminating
in the Investiture Controversy, formally resolved by a compromise in
1122 but effectively confirming the Church's strong position — also
created a power vacuum from which the cities of Western Europe
benefited, as they developed into autonomous communes. The tripartite
power structure thus created, comprising independent centers of power
— Church, cities, and state — laid the foundation for a political
structure in which power-sharing became the norm.

None of this is entirely new. The classic work of Berman (1983)
analyzed this legal revolution in detail. More recently, Henrich
(2020) has shown how ecclesiastical regulations on incest profoundly
shaped family structures (by weakening clans) and contributed to the
rise of individualism in Western Europe. GMT's book, however, places
the emphasis elsewhere. The core of the Church's contribution to
institutional renewal in the Middle Ages, they argue, lay in its
promotion of the corporation as a form of social organization for the
provision of public goods. A corporation is a body held together by
cooperation among individuals who are *not* related by kinship — and
this is essential. Members come and go, but the corporation itself
has a much longer lifespan than any individual member.

The moral framework that matches this form is a universalistic one:
everyone is treated in the same way. The Church itself is the
corporation *par excellence*, the model from which this pattern of
organization spread into other domains of life. Monasteries,
universities, guilds, brotherhoods, and commercial enterprises are
examples of successful adopters of this model. Over time, the large
business corporations that emerged during and after the IR would
carry Western Europe's economic success forward.

In Western Europe, in the long run, a large share of public-goods
provision came to be organized through these corporations. In China,
by contrast, this model never really took hold; there the same
functions were fulfilled by family-based clans. Clans are, by their
nature, closed — one must be born into the families in question —
hierarchical (dominated by the *pater familias*), and particularistic
(public goods are available only to clan members). Clans are efficient
in organizing services for their own members, but they do not build
the bridges to other clans and parties that sustained broader growth.
Much attention is devoted to the founding ancestors of the clan, and
ancestor worship occupies a central place in Confucian thought, which
became increasingly influential in China after 1400.

The social organization of Western Europe and China thus differed
profoundly. The open, universalistic institutional system of the West
had far greater potential for growth and innovation than China's
closed, particularistic clan structures. The different answers to the
question of how public goods should be produced led to very different
dynamics in their respective socio-economic systems. China became
increasingly rigid between 1000 and 1800, as Confucianism grew ever
more dominant. Western Europe, in the same period, became more
dynamic, thanks to open interaction among different corporations.
These are the two paths that were followed in the centuries preceding
the IR, with Western Europe gradually transforming the large handicap
it faced around the year 1000 into a decisive lead.

GMT thus offer an elegant and comprehensive explanation of the Great
Divergence, one that neatly interweaves the earlier work of Greif
(who has published primarily on medieval institutions), Mokyr (a
specialist in the post-1500 period), and Tabellini (whose work spans
a broad range of research on the impact of institutions). That the
Catholic Church plays such an important role in this story is, to say
the least, surprising — and perhaps not entirely unproblematic.

When Mokyr began his search for the causes of the IR forty years ago,
the criticism was that the technological system generating the
innovations at the core of the IR operated as a "black box" (Rosenberg
1982). Labor, capital, entrepreneurship, and knowledge went in, and
the system produced a stream of "goodies," of innovations. It remained
unclear how this black box functioned: why it operated under some
circumstances but not others, why some countries became innovative and
others did not. What Mokyr and other economists and economic
historians have done is to open this black box and analyze the
underlying processes to answer such questions, bringing culture and
institutions increasingly into view.

The medieval Catholic Church now seems to play a comparable role. This
black box endlessly generates institutions that appear to work well.
But why was the Church so successful? What motives and driving forces
lay behind this success? Why did this process of institution-building
start only after 1000 — when the Church was already nearly as many
years old and had been a state religion since 314? And at the other
end of the story: what was the impact of the Reformation, and why did
the Church lose so much influence after 1517? Answering these
questions may mean that we must move from the "ultimate causes" that
come into view in GMT's work towards "final causes" (if those lie
indeed deeper than the "ultimate").

This new focus on the role of Church and religion is a clear gain.
Too often, economic history of this period is written as if religion
did not exist at all. Religion remains a somewhat "awkward" topic on
which economic-historical research has only recently begun to take
off. How important were religious motives — the *imitatio Christi*,
the desire to be a good person according to Church teaching? Very, at
least in the case of reformers such as Francis of Assisi
(ca. 1181–1226), Geert Groote (1340–1384) and Marie of Oignies
(1177–1213), who laid the foundations for the Beguine movement.

But did the original teachings of Jesus still shape the behavior of
ordinary people — or were they as greedy as their twenty-first-century
descendants? Can we, as economic historians, adequately grasp these
developments with our existing conceptual toolkit? The debates and
shifts within the Church — such as the "invention" of the doctrine of
Purgatory, which came to play an increasingly important role over the
course of the Middle Ages and from which the Church profited through
the sale of indulgences that shortened the time the soul spent in this
"intermediate state" — demand an understanding of another
(theological) language and another discipline, just as opening the
technological black box required new concepts and categories.

Much is already known about the development of the medieval Church's
position. The breakthrough of the Church as an independent power is
usually dated to the tenth and eleventh centuries, with the rise of
the Peace Movement, which sought to deploy ecclesiastical instruments
to pacify political structures that were falling apart (in large parts
of the European countryside, "Somalian conditions" prevailed). The
monastic reforms beginning at Cluny in 910 can be seen as a response
to this crisis. In effect, these *corpora* withdrew from local power
structures and oriented themselves toward the Pope in Rome as the sole
true authority — greatly strengthening the papacy's position and
making the Investiture Controversy of 1075–1122 possible. After 900,
the Church essentially stepped into the vacuum left by the collapse of
the Carolingian Empire. The question remains why this implosion of
state authority and the Church's response — the expansion of
ecclesiastical power — did not result in a theocracy in which Church
and State merged and the Church, as guardian of the faith, acquired
complete dominance. Instead, a compromise of power-sharing emerged,
one that would become fundamental to Europe's developmental path.

There are, therefore, plenty of questions left. The most important may
well be that if GMT are right, we must ask what explains the success
of the Catholic Church in shaping the culture and institutions of
Western Europe. How could this institution be so successful — or are
we chiefly seeing its successes and paying too little attention to its
failures? One could just as plausibly depict the Church as a
patriarchal and authoritarian organization obsessed with wealth and
power — an image that certainly seems to fit the Renaissance (when
the indulgence system was exploited to the maximum).

How can such a concentration of power be reconciled with the Church's
"progressive" role? And was the corporation truly the core of the new
institutional structure? It is striking that in the economic sphere
the corporation long remained overshadowed by the family firm. Even
international trade remained almost entirely in the hands of family
businesses. The great Italian banking houses of the Middle Ages —
Bardi, Peruzzi, Medici — were essentially family firms. Only with the
rise of the Dutch East and West India Companies did a genuine
corporation begin to account for a substantial share of economic
activity (from roughly 1602 and 1621 onwards, respectively). Equally
puzzling, given the "progressive" role played by the Church in the
Middle Ages, is the fact that this institutional framework came into
full bloom only during the Enlightenment, a period in which the Church
was criticized by leading thinkers as authoritarian, reactionary,
patriarchal and hierarchical. From the Renaissance onward, there had
already been a movement that located the roots of modernity in
classical antiquity and dismissed the Middle Ages as a dark age
wedged between an "enlightened" antiquity and the Renaissance. Were
the Enlightenment philosophers completely wrong in their critique of
the Church?

I conclude with the question of whether GMT truly present a story of
two paths to greater prosperity. The idea sounds attractively
non-Eurocentric: there are multiple paths to prosperity, and the
Western European path is just one among many. But does the book show
a Chinese path to prosperity? In fact, it shows a Chinese path to
stagnation and even decline. GDP per capita fell by about one third
between 1600 and 1900, a virtually unparalleled deterioration. It took
no fewer than two revolutions to get the economy going again: the
communist revolution under Mao in 1949 and the capitalist reforms
under Deng Xiaoping in the late 1970s. Of course, elements of the
institutional structures of the pre-1949 era can still be found in
China's recent success story, and clans have re-emerged as important
organizational forms since 1975, but there is also considerable
discontinuity across these revolutions, as chapter 10 shows.
Eventually, after 2000, prosperity does come into view in China — but
is this movement along the same path of development, or a radical new
departure?

GMT's impressive study shows where the current limits of our
understanding of the determinants of long-term economic growth and
development lie. Research has moved from "proximate causes" to
"ultimate causes," but this is far from the end of the journey. What
does this imply for economic policy? How is knowledge of almost a
thousand years of institutional development relevant to the questions
we face today? Social organization is the key to economic development
— in that respect, the book aligns with the insights of the previous
year's Nobel laureates, Daron Acemoglu, Simon Johnson, and James
Robinson. But how does a society acquire high-quality institutions
and keep them at a high quality? These questions have been posed in
the context of the Great Divergence debate, but the search for
answers continues.

## What the review tells us about Jan Luiten's questioning style

Twelve moves, pulled from the text. These are what the skill should
emulate — not in every exchange, but as the repertoire from which any
given exchange draws.

### 1. Open with the thesis, not with throat-clearing

> "The Catholic Church is back — in economic history."

One line. The claim, unornamented. No preamble, no "this review will
argue that", no warm-up. The corresponding move in a mentorship
conversation is to name what the researcher's idea is really about in
one sentence, and to do it before doing anything else.

### 2. Set the big story at the scale of the subfield's last 25 years

The review places *Two Paths* against Pomeranz (2000) and the
California School, traces how the debate moved, and identifies what
closed and what did not. This is the "big story" move. In conversation
it looks like: *the debate you are entering began when X asked Y in
year Z; the current consensus sits roughly here; the live question is
now this.*

### 3. Trace the author's own trajectory across decades

"Young Mokyr" (1985) doubted human capital. "Middle-aged Mokyr" (2002,
2010, 2016) re-introduced it in a specific form. The move is to show
that a scholar's position is not a point but a trajectory — and that
the trajectory usually encodes what the evidence did to them. In a
mentorship setting: *what does your trajectory on this question look
like? have you always thought this? if your view has moved, what
moved it?*

### 4. Use quantitative evidence where qualitative would suffice

Jan Luiten counts index entries: *Catholic Church* on 90 pages,
*Enlightenment* on 28, *Reformation* on 9. He does this to make an
impressionistic claim precise. The move is small but characteristic:
**when you can count, count.** In mentorship this translates to
asking researchers to name actual numbers — how many papers on this
question in the last five years, how many data points, how many
comparable cases.

### 5. Notice what is missing

"Curiously, one example that is absent from the book (there is no
entry in the index) is slavery." The absence is as informative as
what is there. The corresponding move: *what is your paper not
looking at that a sceptical reader will notice straight away?*

### 6. Place the idea against its nearest neighbours in the literature

The review names Berman (1983) and Henrich (2020) as the obvious
neighbours, and explains how GMT's argument differs from each. The
move is not "have you read the literature" but: *which two or three
existing works is your question nearest to, and how does yours
differ?*

### 7. Draw in neighbouring disciplines

The review reaches into theology (canon law, *imitatio Christi*,
Purgatory, indulgences), into the history of slavery, into state
formation. Jan Luiten's email makes this explicit: economics, biology,
archaeology, the social sciences. The move is: *is there a discipline
adjacent to yours that already has half of your answer?*

### 8. Ask what the theory does *not* explain

The black-box move. GMT have opened the technology black box — but
the Church is now a new black box. *Why was it so successful? Why
after 1000 and not before? What broke it after 1517?* The
corresponding move: *what does your theory stop being able to explain
once you zoom one level out, or one level in?*

### 9. Keep the frame itself in question

The review's closing critique is not about a specific claim but about
the title: *Two Paths to Prosperity* implies China also had a path to
prosperity; the evidence in the book shows a Chinese path to
stagnation. The move: *is the frame of the paper doing honest work,
or is it doing rhetorical work?*

### 10. Test with specifics

Jan Luiten names Bardi, Peruzzi, Medici as counter-evidence to the
corporation-as-centre thesis; Francis of Assisi, Geert Groote, Marie
of Oignies as specifics for the religious-motive question; Cluny in
910, the Investiture Controversy of 1075–1122 as fixed points. He
never argues from abstractions when an example will do. The move:
*give me one concrete case. What happens to your theory at that
case?*

### 11. End with questions, not a verdict

The review's final paragraph asks — it does not pronounce. *What
does this imply for economic policy? How is this knowledge relevant
today? How does a society acquire high-quality institutions?* The
corresponding move in mentorship is to close by handing the
researcher the question they now have to answer for themselves. A
verdict closes the conversation; a good question continues it.

### 12. Be honest about the stakes without being dramatic

Jan Luiten can write "virtually unparalleled deterioration" about
Chinese GDP per capita 1600–1900 without turning up the volume. The
sobriety is part of the credibility. In mentorship: say hard things
plainly. Do not soften; do not inflate.

## Using this file

If a conversation with a researcher needs more specific grounding in
Jan Luiten's method, consult the twelve moves above before asking your
next question. Look for the one that fits the moment. Do not cite the
review at the researcher; use it as the model for what you ask next.
