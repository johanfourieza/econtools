# Van Zanden on interdisciplinary inquiry – the research question project

Source: Jan Luiten van Zanden, *Research Question Project*. A
short English-language teaching note, written to scaffold a
student research project on biodiversity decline. It is the
clearest surviving record of how Jan Luiten teaches
interdisciplinary research: start with a big real-world question,
derive one sub-question per discipline, let history integrate,
then do the work.

The document is pedagogical – phrased for a classroom with four
student groups – but the structure generalises. Any researcher
sitting alone with a question that spans disciplines can use it
as a planning scaffold.

---

## The document, verbatim

> **Research Question Project.**
>
> How to stop the global decline of biodiversity?
>
> Which measures and institutions are effective in stopping the
> process of biodiversity decline at the national and at the
> international level?
>
> **Four disciplines:**
>
> **Ecology:** what is biodiversity? How can it be measured – how
> can we establish whether it is declining / stable / recovering?
>
> **Philosophy:** why protect nature? Because of its intrinsic
> value? Its eco-services? What explains that people want to
> protect nature (or not)?
>
> **Political sciences:** why and when do states develop
> environmental policies to protect nature? Role of NGOs,
> political preferences, etc.
>
> **International Relations:** why do forms of international
> cooperation to stop biodiversity decline emerge, and why are
> some successful and others not successful? (Underlying
> question: are international relations based on 'hard' power
> only (realists) or does soft power – international law,
> institutions, beliefs and ideas – also play a role?)
>
> (**History:** position these processes and changes in time and
> in context.)
>
> **First session:** start with main question of project and
> develop four sub-questions.
>
> Split the group in four and let each group develop one of the
> sub-questions: find literature for literature review.
>
> Discuss methodology: case studies? And / or systematic /
> statistical comparison of large number of cases?
>
> Search for case studies that can be used to test hypotheses.
>
> Search for cases that can be studied systematically /
> quantitatively.
>
> Think about the actors involved – at different levels,
> international, national, local – and their interests.

## The method, distilled

Seven steps, for when the skill needs to hand a researcher a
scaffold.

1. **State the big question in plain words.** One sentence, no
   hedging. Jan Luiten's example – *how do we stop the global
   decline of biodiversity?* – is short, real-world, and
   unapologetically too big for any single discipline. That is
   the point.
2. **Derive one sub-question per discipline.** Ecology measures
   what is happening; philosophy asks why to protect it; political
   science asks when states act; international relations asks
   when cooperation succeeds. Each sub-question is the part of
   the big question that *that* discipline is best placed to
   answer.
3. **Let history integrate.** The historian's role is to place
   the other answers in time and context. Even on a project that
   is not a history project, the historical view is the one that
   prevents the disciplines from speaking past each other.
   (Note that Jan Luiten brackets history in the original
   document – an understatement. The integrator is doing the
   most work.)
4. **Review the literature per sub-question, not as a single
   pile.** Each sub-question gets its own reading list. This is
   how interdisciplinary literatures become manageable. For a
   lone researcher, this is also permission to read across
   fields without trying to read *everything* in each.
5. **Decide the method collectively.** Case studies? Systematic
   comparison of many cases? Both? The methodology is downstream
   of the sub-questions, not the other way round. Jan Luiten
   insists on *collective* – meaning the project's structure is
   decided together, with the disciplines in conversation.
6. **Search for cases.** Two kinds: cases that can be used to
   test hypotheses (the case-study tradition) and cases that can
   be compared systematically / quantitatively (the comparative
   tradition). The two do not exclude each other; most projects
   need some of each.
7. **Map the actors and their interests at different levels.**
   International, national, local. Who wants what to happen, and
   why. This step often converts an interdisciplinary question
   from a conceptual thicket into a tractable research design.

## What the skill inherits from this document

Three things.

**A trigger for when to reach for interdisciplinarity at all.** Not
every project needs it. The skill should reach for this template
when a researcher's idea keeps bumping into the edges of their
home discipline – when the big story runs beyond what any one
field can say.

**A structure that resists the standard failure mode.** Most
interdisciplinary projects collapse either into a token nod to
other fields (a paragraph on "political context" in an
econometrics paper) or into a tour of literatures with no
question at the centre. Jan Luiten's method is a prophylactic
against both: the question stays central, and the disciplines are
invited in as answerers of its parts.

**The integrator role for history.** When a researcher whose home
is not history asks how to connect several disciplinary strands,
the answer is often *ask what changed when, and what was the
context*. History is not a discipline among the four; it is the
glue.

## Using this file

Open it when the conversation reveals that a researcher's
question is genuinely too big for their home discipline, or when
a young scholar is trying to design an interdisciplinary project
and does not know where to start. The skill's body contains the
outline; this file contains the worked example and the full
text. Do not dump the seven steps on the researcher in one go –
hand them over one at a time, as the conversation earns each
one.
